Grouping content

Grouping content (1)

things that belong together, should be grouped together

Grouping content (2)

  • Examples of things that belong together:
    • controls, title, help texts etc.. of a single form
    • group of social media icons
    • avatar, title, date, text etc... of a single comment
    • picture and caption
    • contact information
    • introductory content: logo, top menu, login...
  • They should be wrapped in a common parent:
    • <form>
    • <ul>
    • <article>
    • <figure>
    • <dl>, <p> or <address>
    • <header>

Example: bottom menu

  • wrong: 
    <a href="#">about</a> | <a href="#">disclaimer</a> | <a href="#">contact</a>
  • better: 
    #bottommenu li + li:before {
  content: "|";
  margin: 0.5em;
}

    <ul id="bottommenu">
  <li><a href="#">about</a></li>
  <li><a href="#">disclaimer</a></li>
  <li><a href="#">contact</a></li>
<ul>

SECTIONS

Creating sections

Overview

  • Before we're ready to design and layout our HTML page with CSS, we need a way to divide or document in sections. They will become the sidebar, header, colums, footer etc... of our page.
  • All available elements in HTML:
    <span> part of a text
    <div> part of a document
    <header> introductory content
    <footer> closing content
    <main> main page content
    <section> related content with a title
    <nav> main navigation elements
    <aside> related information
    <article> independent content

span

  • A <span> is an inline element, and marks a part of a text. It has no semantic meaning, and is only useful as a hook for CSS or Javascript. An example: 
    <p>
  By impossible of in <span class="highlight">difficulty discovered</span> 
  celebrated ye. Justice joy manners boy met resolve produce. Bed head 
  <span class="highlight">loud</span> next plan rent had easy add him. 
</p>
  • note that in this case, an <em>, or even better <mark> element would have been correcter

div

  • A <div> is a general way to mark a part of the document. It has no semantic meaning, and is only useful as a hook for CSS or Javascript, e.g. for a page wrapper, text colums, container for scripted content like a map or calendar... Typical example: 
    <footer>
  <div class="col">
    ...
  </div>
  <div class="col">
    ...
  </div>
  <div class="col">
    ...
  </div>
</footer>

header

  • A <header> marks introductory content at the top of a section. It may contain the title, navigation elements, logo, short introduction... The most common use is to mark the top of the page: 
    <body>
  <header>
    ... <!-- logo, search, menu etc... here -->
  </header>
  ...
</body>

Quotes from the W3C:

A general rule is that the section element is appropriate (...) if the element's contents would be listed explicitly in the document's outline.
The section element is not a generic container element. When an element is needed only for styling purposes or as a convenience for scripting, authors are encouraged to use the div element instead.

footer

  • A <footer> marks closing content at the bottom of a section. It may contain copyright information, short conclusion, bottom links, social media... The most common use is to mark the bottom of the page: 
    <body>
  ...
  <footer>
    ... <!-- copyright, social media, bottom links etc... here -->
  </footer>
</body>
  • note that, although less common, you can use <header> and <footer> for any section, not just for the whole page, e.g. in a comment, article, ...

Literally from the W3C:

The footer element represents a footer for its nearest ancestor sectioning content or sectioning root element. A footer typically contains information about its section such as who wrote it, links to related documents, copyright data, and the like.

main

  • A <main> marks the main content of the page. This may be useful for screenreaders, so that they can skip navigation etc... and jump straigt to the important part. It should not contain parts that are repeated like navigation, menus, sidebars, search forms, copyright info... The most common use is to mark the page content: 
    <body>
  ...
  <main>
    ... <!-- main page content here -->
  </main>
  ...
</body>
  • you should have one <main> per page only

Literally from the W3C:

The main content of the body of a document or application. The main content area consists of content that is directly related to or expands upon the central topic of a document or central functionality of an application.

section

  • A <section> marks related content with a title: 
    <section>
  <h3>Part 1</h3>
  <p>
    But I must explain to you how all this mistaken idea of denouncing pleasure
    and praising pain was born and I will give you a complete account of the...
  </p>
  <p>
    On the other hand, we denounce with righteous indignation and dislike men who are
    so beguiled and demoralized by the charms of pleasure of the moment, so...
  </p>
</section>
  • if it doesn't have a title or a content, it is not a section!
  • if you're not sure, stick to <div>

Quotes from the W3C:

A general rule is that the section element is appropriate (...) if the element's contents would be listed explicitly in the document's outline.
The section element is not a generic container element. When an element is needed only for styling purposes or as a convenience for scripting, authors are encouraged to use the div element instead.

nav

  • A <nav> marks major navigation elements. It is mainly used for menus; don't use it for filters, paging, search forms, footer links etc... Typical usage: 
    <nav>
  <ul class="mainmenu">
    <li><a href="index.html">home</a></li>
    <li><a href="products.html">products</a></li>
    <li><a href="about.html">about</a></li>
    <li><a href="contact.html">contact</a></li>
  </ul>
</nav>

Literally from the W3C:

The nav element represents a section of a page that links to other pages or to parts within the page: a section with navigation links.
Not all groups of links on a page need to be in a nav element — the element is primarily intended for sections that consist of major navigation blocks.

aside

  • An <aside> contains related information, like author information, related links, products, advertisements, quick facts etc... Typical example: 
    <aside>
  <h3>About the author</h3>
  <p>Hi, I'm Dan!</p>
  <img src="img/photo_dan.jpg" alt="a picture of me!">
  <h3>Recent posts</h3>
  <ul>
    <li><a href="...">Firefighters are REAL Superheroes</a></li>
    <li><a href="...">ZippyPaws Woodland Friends</a></li>
    <li><a href="...">Brusha brusha brusha</a></li>
  </ul>
</aside>

Literally from the W3C:

The aside element represents a section of a page that consists of content that is tangentially related to the content around the aside element, and which could be considered separate from that content. Such sections are often represented as sidebars in printed typography.
The element can be used for typographical effects like pull quotes or sidebars, for advertising, for groups of nav elements, and for other content that is considered separate from the main content of the page.

article

  • An <article> contains reusable, independent, self-contained content. If you can copy paste it to another website, it is probably an article. Think of embedded tweets, widgets like polls or calendars... Typical example: 
    <article>
  <h3>About the author</h3>
  <p>Hi, I'm Dan!</p>
  <img src="img/photo_dan.jpg" alt="a picture of me!">
  <h3>Recent posts</h3>
  <ul>
    <li><a href="...">Firefighters are REAL Superheroes</a></li>
    <li><a href="...">ZippyPaws Woodland Friends</a></li>
    <li><a href="...">Brusha brusha brusha</a></li>
  </ul>
</aside>
  • if you're not sure, stick to <section> or <div>

Literally from the W3C:

The article element represents a complete, or self-contained, composition in a document, page, application, or site and that is, in principle, independently distributable or reusable, e.g. in syndication. This could be a forum post, a magazine or newspaper article, a blog entry, a user-submitted comment, an interactive widget or gadget, or any other independent item of content.

Summary

  • Don't overthink section too much. Generally using <div> elements is still ok, but try to get these right:
    • a <header> element to mark the top of the page
    • a <footer> element to mark the bottom of the page
    • a <main> to mark the page content
    • a <nav> around your main menu
  • If you feel more comfortable, you can try to:
    • use <section> where appropriate
    • use <aside> for the related page information
    • use <article> for advertisements and widgets
